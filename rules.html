ROOK

  • The rook moves along ranks (rows) and files (columns) on the chessboard.
  • It can move in either a horizontal or vertical direction.
  • The rook cannot jump over other pieces. If there's a piece in its path, it can't move past it.
  • The rook can capture an opponent's piece by moving to a square occupied by an enemy piece.
  • A rook can be involved in a special move called castling, where it moves together with the king to improve the king's safety.

KNIGHT

  • The knight moves in an L-shape: two squares in one direction and then one square in a perpendicular direction.
  • It can jump over other pieces, landing on a square occupied by an opponent's piece.
  • Its movement is unique and less predictable than other pieces.
  • Capturing involves moving directly to the square of the opponent's piece.
  • The knight's movement can create tactical opportunities like forks and pins.

BISHOP

  • The bishop's path traces diagonal lines on the board.
  • It can move any number of squares diagonally, making it versatile in long-range movements.
  • Due to its starting position, a bishop always remains on squares of the same color throughout the game..
  • Unlike other pieces, the bishop is restricted to moving diagonally only; it cannot move horizontally or vertically.
  • When capturing, the bishop replaces the opponent's piece on the square it moves to.

QUEEN

  • The queen moves both diagonally and in straight lines across the board.
  • It can move any number of squares in any direction, making it the most powerful piece..
  • The queen's movement combines the abilities of both the bishop and the rook.
  • Similar to other pieces, it cannot jump over other pieces and can only move to empty squares or capture opponent's pieces.
  • When capturing, the queen moves to the square occupied by an opponent's piece.

KING

  • The king can move one square in any direction: horizontally, vertically, or diagonally.
  • It cannot move to a square that would put it under threat or in check.
  • The king's safety is crucial; if it's under attack and no legal moves are available, the game ends..
  • Like other pieces, the king cannot jump over pieces and must move to an empty square or capture an opponent's piece.
  • When capturing, the king moves to the square occupied by an opponent's piece.

PAWN

  • Pawns move forward one square, but capture diagonally.
  • On its first move, a pawn can advance two squares.
  • Pawns capture by moving one square diagonally forward.
  • Pawns can capture "en passant" when an opponent's pawn advances two squares from its starting position.
  • When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board, it can promote to any other piece (except a king).
